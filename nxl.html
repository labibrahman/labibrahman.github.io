Question 2: What is the difference between 3G, UMTS, 4G, HSPA+, LTE, WCDMA, EVDO

Short Version

3G and 4G are blanket terms applied to mobile networks that meet 3G and 4G specifications as defined by the International Telecommunication Union.


UMTS and WCDMA are the network architecture and air interface used by GSM networks to meet 3G standards while CDMA networks use EVDO as the air interface.


HSPA+ is a improvement on top of WCDMA that delivers higher upload and download speeds. It requires no hardware modification on towers or handsets. LTE is a UMTS based architecture that allows carriers to meet 4G standards. It is used by both GSM and CDMA based carriers and requires hardware upgrades on towers and smartphones.



Nuanced Version

Wireless Network Basics

Let's start from the beginning.


In the United States, all communication via radio waves is governed by the FCC. Wireless carriers such as T-Mobile therefore by definition fall under their purview. The FCC assigns each wireless carrier a System Identification Code or (SID). Towers operated by carriers constantly broadcast their SIDs.


A cellphone with an active wireless contract knows the SID of the carrier it is authorized to use. When a phone is first powered on, it first checks for see if its authorized SID is being broadcasted by a tower. If there's a positive match, the phone sends the customer's information to the tower. The tower checks the information and if all goes well, the phone is now connected to the network.


Here's where standards come into play. GSM networks like ATT store the SID and other information necessary to carry out this handshake on a removable chip called a SIM card that is inserted into phones. cdmaOne networks like Verizon on the other hand, hard code this information into specific devices.


The protocol used by GSM networks to talk to towers, also known as the "air interface" is called TDMA. cdmaOne networks use an air interface conveniently called CDMA.


This GSM/TDMA and cdmaOne/CDMA based first iteration of fully digital wireless telephone networks was called 2G. As wireless networks matured, there was a need to create a set of standards to apply to networks as they advanced to ensure global consistency and prevent customer confusion.


Going to 3G

Accordingly, the International Teleommunication Union (ITU) created a set of standards called IMT-2000 that when met would classify a network as a third generation, or 3G network. 3G is therefore a blanket term used to describe wireless networks that meet IMT-2000 specifications. The technical specifications of IMT-2000 require a 3G network to provide users with minimum speeds of 2Mbps when stationary and 348Kbps when in a moving vehicle. The 2G network protocols weren't technically capable of meeting 3G standards. Two bodies, 3GPP and 3GPP2,were created to provide an upgrade path for GSM and cdmaOne to 3G standards.


In order to upgrade to 3G, GSM carriers converted to a protocol called UMTS with an air interface called WCDMA. cdmaOne carriers converted to CDMA2000 with an air interface called CDMA2000 1xRTT.


Carriers however were interested in further increasing speeds. UMTS networks developed a new air interface on top of WCDMA called HSPA+ that provided theoretical maximum download speeds of 168Mbps. CDMA2000 networks upgraded their air interfaces from 1xRTT to EVDO.


4G and confusion

Meanwhile, the ITU developed a new set of specifications to classify 4G networks and called it IMT-Advanced. 4G networks that met IMT-Advanced specifications would have to use IP-based packet switching and have peak speeds of 1Gbps when stationary, and 100Mbps when in a moving vehicle.


The 3GPP developed LTE as the upgrade path for both UMTS and CDMA2000 based carriers. The earlierst deployments of LTE however failed to meet the speed requirements defined by IMT-Advanced.


Interestingly, despite their early LTE deployments not meeting IMT-Advanced specifications, Verizon and ATT began branding their new LTE networks as 4G. Much to their gall, T-Mobile responded by branding its HSPA+ coverage as 4G.


Caving to all the confusion caused by the branding, the ITU decided to allow classification of HSPA+ and early deployed LTE as 4G despite their not meeting IMT-Advanced standards.


LTE-Advanced which is currently being deployed by the major US carriers does meet IMT-Advanced specifications and will be classified as "true 4G" by the ITU. Interestingly, LTE is deployed on top of UMTS which means that CDMA2000 carriers like Verizon now have to include SIM cards in phones that connect to its LTE network.

What is the difference between and MVNO and a regional carrier?

An MVNO owns no network infrastructure. Instead it leases bandwidth from an operator at wholesale prices and resells it to customers through service plans.


On the other hand, regional carriers own network infrastructure, but only in specific geographic regions. To provide customers with service outside of those regions, they often negotiate roaming agreements with carriers who have coverage there.

What is one Machine-to-Machine enterprise application? What pieces do you need to assemble a complete solution for that customer?

The most famous consumer facing M2M application is probably WhisperSync. WhisperSync is Amazon's name for the wireless sync service it bundles with Kindle devices. Kindle devices in the US connect for free to Amazon's servers through AT&T's network. Through this connection, Kindle users can buy new Kindle books and have them delivered immediately to their Kindle. They can also sync read position and bookmarks across multiple platforms.


Pieces needed:

  • Hardware: If the customer is seeking a solution for their own hardware, then this takes the form of providing a radio chipset. Otherwise, custom hardware will have to be design to meet cusomer needs
  • Connectivity: Contracts will have to be negotiated with carriers with regards to per device data buckets as well as coverage.
  • Software: For a complete solution, software could be provided so that the only work on the customer's end is set up a server endpoint that is called by the devices.
  • Support: Servicing and replacing hardware, addressing connectivity issues, ensuring physical security of the hardware.
